# Preface

After two attempts at penning a preface for this story — a story which, as of the time of this
writing, consists of no more than a title — I find myself faced with the fundamental question, "Why
write a preface in the first place?" The longer I think about it, the less I want to (the less I
want to keep thinking about it, that is). It's leading me to such questions as "Why even write a
story?" and "Why do anything at all?" and "Why do I need a reason for everything I do?" Maybe those
are all valid questions though, and maybe answering some of them is as good a premise for a preface
as any.

So let's start with the first one, and willfully forget about the others. Yes, why am I writing this
story? Surely there are better uses of my time and creative faculties. I could, for example, take on
some software project, thereby improving my programming skills while having some fun at the same
time. I could also write a song, earnestly expressing some human emotion and enjoying the sweet
sound of music. There are probably an infinite number of ways, in fact, that I could spend my time
making something new, each with valid reasons for pursuing them.

Earlier today though, I got to thinking that it had been a while since I created something with the
sole intention of enjoying making it. So much of what I had recently created had some other
motivation behind it, somewhat removed from the goal of my own amusement in the process. In fact, I
couldn't even remember the last time I engaged in that kind of activity. Naturally I wanted to find
one, and preferably one that I could start working on as soon as possible.

{/** I started cycling through potential creative projects in my mind, judging each on the criteria
of, simply put, "Would that be awesome?" I considered digging into another Project Euler problem.
That would definitely provide a fun outlet to write something and also to think mathematically, but
it wasn't quite right. Part of the reason I tackle those problems is to keep my mind sharp and
refresh my math skills, and while those aren't bad goals in the slightest, I wanted to do something
that, to as high a degree as possible, did not need to provide any secondary benefits to keep me
interested. Some similar ideas came to me (for example, analyzing the color palettes of fantasy art
pieces on Reddit and writing a song about the Ride of the Rohirrim) but they too were missing the
self-evident awesomeness I was after. **/}

Now, as you might have guessed by the title of this narrative (and the post which contains it), I
ultimately found {/* *a solution to my quandary* */} the creative outlet I was after in deciding to
write _A Short Story About a Wizard_. Maybe it's the fact that I've just finished reading about some
of Gandalf's behind-the-scenes deeds in J.R.R. Tolkien's _Unfinished Tales of Númenor and
Middle-earth_ (fantastically compiled and annotated by his son Christopher), or maybe it's because
I've been enjoying the memes over at [r/wizardposting](https://www.reddit.com/r/wizardposting/), but
I am quite looking forward to putting my own slice of wizbiz into writing.

Do I have a clue what the plot will be? The setting? Other characters? Absolutely not. What I do
have though is the spirit: the spirit of wanting to tell a cool story for the singular purpose of
telling a cool story. I don't know if that will be enough to actually get me through it, but as far
as I'm concerned, I don't want to do it for any other reason. So, I hope you do enjoy, and without
further ado...

# A Short Story About a Wizard

"This is a short story about a wizard," is an opening line, and an exciting one at that. It's as
equally rich with possibility and mystery as it is in need of an actual story to substantiate the
claim. All excitement aside, however, it's the kind of line that could be read incorrectly in
certain circumstances. Imagine, for example, a reader so overwhelmingly excited to be reading a
story about a wizard, that in his haste he mucks up the opening statement as, "This is a story about
a short wizard."

Now, it's quite obvious really that these two statements are by no means equivalent, neither from a
visual nor logical perspective, and in many cases in fact (but far from all), the one should not be
confused with the other, for such an error would certainly lead to utter confusion as the
subsequently unfolding story fails to meet expectations. It may come as a surprise to you then, that
this tale is one such exception to the rule, a case in which the syntactical error described above
will have little to no bearing on the reader's level of bamboozlement.

That is to say, that just as a short story is a story nonetheless, Paul, the protagonist of our
short story, was himself of similar stature (that is, quite short). Indeed, his magical fellows
often stood at least two heads taller than he, an easy three if casting _levitate_ on themselves,
and it was not uncommon for ordinary folk to mistake him for a shriveled, weak geezer. It should be
noted that, at least for the part of being shriveled and of being a geezer, they weren't at all
wrong — the judgment was understandable even, given his bushy, snow-white beard and the knotted old
cane that often bore more of his weight than it cared to. But for the assessment of his capability,
well, they didn't quite hit they mark. Even among those who practiced magic (a rather discreet bunch
given the strict laws and harsh punishments surrounding wizardry in that age), those that could
recognize Paul for what he truly was were few and far between.

While the scraggly unkemptness of his beard was well in accordance with practically every other
detail of his outward appearance, from his dingy, worn-out robes to his tattered leather boots, this
all contrasted sharply against his naturally well-natured eyebrows (although they too had long ago
turned silvery white), which curled evenly upwards, just barely brushing the brim of his faded blue
wizard hat when at rest. Whether or not they provided some kind of sixth sense is anyone's guess,
but when the eyes above which they grew were getting a read on a situation, they often twiddled up
and down, out of sync, like radio antenna tuning into some secret broadcast about whatever scene was
soon to play out.
