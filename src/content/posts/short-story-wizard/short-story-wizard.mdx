# Preface

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;After two attempts at penning a preface for this story — a story
which, as of the time of this writing, consists of no more than a title — I find myself asking the
fundamental question, "Why write a preface in the first place?" The longer I think about it, the
less I want to (the less I want to keep thinking about it, that is). It's leading me to such
questions as "Why even write a story?" and "Why do anything at all?" and "Why do I need a reason for
everything I do?" Maybe those are all valid questions though, and maybe answering some of them is as
good a premise for a preface as any.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;So let's start with the first one, and willfully forget about the
others. Yes, out of all the ways I could spend my time, out of every channel of creative effort, why
in the world should I craft a story? It won't help my career, and it almost certainly won't generate
any income. I'm fairly confident, in fact, that hardly anybody besides myself will ever read it. In
other words, it would be negligibly beneficial to me beyond my own personal delight in writing and
reading it.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Well as a matter of fact, that's exactly why I want to do it. It's
recently occurred to me that it's been a while since I made something with the sole intention of
enjoying making it, with practically all of my creative endeavors as of late having had some other
motivations behind them, a degree or two removed from my own amusement in them. In fact, I can't
even remember the last time I engaged in something purely for its own purpose.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;So, to remedy the lack of autotelic projects in my life, and as you
might have guessed by the title of this narrative, I've ultimately found such a creative outlet in
deciding to write _A Short Story About a Wizard_. Maybe it's the fact that I've just finished
reading about some of Gandalf's behind-the-scenes deeds in J.R.R. Tolkien's _Unfinished Tales of
Númenor and Middle-earth_ (fantastically compiled and annotated by his son Christopher), or maybe
it's because I've been enjoying the memes over at
[r/wizardposting](https://www.reddit.com/r/wizardposting/), but I am really looking forward to
putting my own slice of wizbiz into writing.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Do I have a clue what the plot will be? The setting? Other characters?
Absolutely not. What I do have though is the spirit: the spirit of wanting to tell a cool story for
the singular purpose of telling a cool story. I don't know if that will be enough of a reason to
actually get me through it, but as far as I'm concerned, that's the only one I want for to do it.
So, without further ado, and I hope you do enjoy (although for the sake of honesty, and to
contradict that bit about abstaining from further ado, it's really neither here nor there to me
whether you enjoy it or not — all that about writing it for myself and such), this is...

# A Short Story About a Wizard

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"This is a short story about a wizard," is an opening line, and an
exciting one at that. Much like a positive affirmation, the kind that one asserts before the
bathroom mirror as he tries to convince himself that "Today will be a fantastic day, and I will be
open to whatever it brings," it's as rich with possibility and mystery as it is in need of further
content to substantiate the claim (that content of course being more, relevant lines in the case of
the story about the wizard, and an ensuing day that is in fact fantastic in the case of the daily
affirmation). As it just so happens, the opening statement declared above does aptly describe this
story, contrary to its current proceedings, and further, this story begins, or continues rather,
with the very affirmation to which its opening line was just compared:

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"Today will be a fantastic day, and I will be open to whatever it
brings," Lawrence Jennings beamed at his fogged-out reflection in the bathroom mirror, hands perched
pointedly on his bony hips and feet planted firmly on the floor — a "power position" he had been
taught in wizarding school to improve his confidence before duels. He never did perform too terribly
well when it came to dueling (in fact he performed, well, terribly), to the effect that his present
posture, rather than providing him any actual advantage in his matches, only ever served to help him
lose looking misguidedly self-assured that he would not. Nevertheless, he persisted the habit of
holding the stance daily after showering, a practice which, along with the repetition of some
positive affirmation to himself, offered about as much improvement to his everyday affairs as it had
to his dueling ability. Undeterred by past experience, he pressed on.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"Today will be a fantastic day, and I will be open to whatever it
brings." This time he said it a bit more strongly, lifting slightly from his heels onto his toes —
though still not quite reaching a height at which he'd wish to stand normally — and pushing his
scrawny chest outward. He almost convinced himself now that this day would be a great one and
further that he would seize its rewards gladly, but this was unlikely to be the case, given the
record of those that came before it. See, after his time in the wizarding academy, complete with the
typical troubles of adolescence — bullying, heartbreak, unmet expectations, etc. — but holding the
promise of a brighter future, Lawrence had the all-too-common experience of taking a job of which he
was not particularly fond.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;He was a deadferry, that is, one who is (under)compensated for the
necessary yet unsavory task of expelling the souls of the deceased from this world to the next,
particularly those that for some reason could not, or in many cases could not be bothered to, get
along with it. Whether that next place was a good one (a position widely held by the masses) or a
bad one, or even a blissful oblivion, nobody truly was certain; what was certain, though, was that
the souls had to go, and somebody had to see to their going.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The need for their ferrying wasn't so attributable to any danger they
posed to society, for the extent to which they could interact with the living directly was really
quite miniscule, but it was more so due to their general presentation as a nuisance. As one might
imagine, the state of being dead while simultaneously not having the capability or motivation to
move on from this world was not a very cheerful one, and as such ghosts were often liable to
complain — rather loudly — about anything and everything around them. Further, as they reflected
visually the overall character of the persons they were before arriving at their current undead
dispositions, such that the soul of a truly kind person, for example, might appear as a beautiful
sight to the eyes of the living, ghosts were by and far fairly ugly. They typically weren't
grotesque by any means, but if you imagine the soul of an average person, you'll get the point.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Lastly and perhaps most poignantly, all ghosts, including even the
prettiest and most handsome among them, smelled absolutely awful. Their distinctive stench was
unmistakably that of death, yet it also carried with it a host of other rancid odors, these being
transient and ever-changing, to the effect that a nose with the misfortune of smelling them could
never quite adjust to their source. Just as some hypothetical member of the living was getting
acclimated to a nearby ghost's ungodly funk, resembling for example the reek of a feline-infested
back alleyway — then boom — a whiff of booze-binge, pub-fare flatulence would hit them square in the
nostrils, only to be followed up soon after by some other terrible smell, and inevitably another
after that, all underpinned by the droning disaroma of mortal decay.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Anyway, knowing full well that the day ahead of him would consist
almost entirely of coaxing obnoxious, unsightly and above all infragrant apparitions into
relinquishing their ties to this domain, with perhaps the chance to aid some poor yet nonetheless
smelly soul in gaining the closure that would free her from her ghostly shackles, Lawrence
towel-dried his curly red hair, struck once more his previous pose and, looking directly into the
faint blue eyes of his freckled (and according, often in word and sometimes in action, to many of
his prior classmates, punchable) face, shouted with sincere determination, "Today will be a
fantastic day, and I will be open to whatever it brings!"

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"No it won't," chimed in a characteristically indistinct voice from
beyond the bathroom door, "and neither should you."

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"What do you mean by that?" Lawrence snapped back, caught off guard
that his familiar had heard him. "I must've gotten carried away with my affirmations again," he
thought to himself.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"You're going to have a bad day, and if you know what's good for you,
you shouldn't be receptive to anything it has in store," remarked the voice. Lawrence wrapped his
towel around his waist and opened the door to face his familiar, a sentient, nebulous blob of
indeterminate color, its boundaries defined by their very indefinitude, without a hint of a
distinguishing feature to be found. More of an *un*familiar, if you will. In any case, Lawrence
looked his familiar dead in the face, or at least, dead in the place he supposed its face would have
been had it actually had one, and said,

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"Ernest, you're wrong. I can feel it my bones. Today is going to be
different."

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"You say that every day."

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"I know, I know. This is the one though. This is the day everything is
going to turn around for me. Just you watch."

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;"Alright," said the hazy figure as its master walked literally
straight through it — benefits of being the master, "but I don't want to hear it when things
inevitably turn out like they always do." The projection of its voice tracked away from the bathroom
and towards Lawrence as he stepped into the kitchen of his one-bedroom apartment.
